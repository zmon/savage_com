Encouraging Positive Attitudes Can Enhance Your Resilience

Posted on December 15, 2010 by Jerry Sheridan


Highlights:

Resilient actions and resilient attitudes are centrally important to becoming more resilient.

In this post we want to call your attention to the value of optimism.

Research on the influence of optimism and pessimism has tended to be focused on three things:

  • Job and Athletic Performance.
  • Relationship Success.
  • Health.

***************************************************************************

A major way to improve your resilience is to learn the kinds of self-soothing skills we have been discussing, and turning them into habits. Developing habits of self-soothing in distressing situations and of relaxing deeply to replenish yourself are pivotal to improving resilience.

Equally important is shifting how you think, your attitudes. Positive attitudes improve resilience.

More resilient people:

  • Are optimistic, consider interpretations of life events that are positive.
  • See problems as solvable.
  • See their lives as making sense, as meaningful.
  • Feel that they have valuable connections to others.

Resilient actions and resilient attitudes are centrally important to becoming more resilient.

In this post we want to call your attention to the value of optimism.

Research on the influence of optimism and pessimism has tended to be focused on three things:

  • Job and Athletic Performance.
  • Relationship Success.
  • Health.

To provide some examples:

Job Success

  • Optimists tend to get higher overall job performance ratings from their supervisors.
  • In a study of optimistic versus pessimistic insurance agents, the optimists sold more, were given higher ratings by managers, and were less likely to quit their jobs.
  • Tests of optimism and pessimism given during orientation sessions for new production workers at a manufacturing facility predicted later supervisor performance ratings. Pessimism was a particularly strong predictor of poorer performance.

Athletic Performance

  • Optimistic athletes tend to perform better than pessimists.
  • Optimistic athletes tend to sustain their performance better than pessimists when faced with adversity, e.g. being behind behind in a game or playing a tough competitor after you have not performed up to expectations in a previous event.

Level of Physical Health and Coping with Illness

  • Optimists tend be more healthy than pessimists.
  • Optimists recover more quickly from major surgery, return to work sooner, end up with a higher post-surgical quality of life, and are less likely to be re-hospitalized.
  • Optimists have a higher quality of life and better biological markers of recovery if they undergo chemotherapy.
  • Optimists have a lower risk of all causes of death, especially death due to heart disease.
  • If required to become a long-term caregiver for a loved one who is seriously ill, optimists stay healthier, and the person they are caring for does too.
  • Pessimists are more likely than optimists to die prematurely.

Mental Health

  • Optimists are less likely to be depressed.
  • Optimists tend to be less anxious.
  • Optimists are more likely to have high satisfaction with their lives.
  • Optimistic mothers are less likely to suffer from post-partum depression.
  • Optimists tend to have more energy than pessimists.

Success in Relationships

  • Optimists tend to be more satisfied with their primary relationship.
  • Optimists who discuss one of their conflicts see the discussion as more constructive and, later, feel the conflict was better resolved.
  • Optimistic women treated for breast cancer feel more supported and more physically attractive than pessimists.

These bulleted points are not mere opinions. They are all based in scientific studies, many of them available on line. Yet some prominent writers deny the value of optimism. We will take a close look at their objections in the next post.
