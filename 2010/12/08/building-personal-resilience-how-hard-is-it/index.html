Building Personal Resilience: How Hard Is It?

Posted on December 8, 2010 by Jerry Sheridan

Highlights:

When the exercise came to an end, I realized that it had produced far more than merely “taking the edge off the anxiety”. I was downright serene. Somehow, my entire perspective had shifted.

**********************************************************

We strongly advocate devoting a good deal of time and energy to building your resilience, so you may be surprised to learn that we also believe that:

  • You can improve it with very little effort. You can make significant improvements in your resilience by practicing a few minutes per day.
  • You can even use resilience-enhancing “prosthetic procedures” or “props” that require little or no practice, though their usefulness gets greater with use.

Props function in a way that is similar to the way eyeglasses or canes do. Virtually anyone can use them, and they help you function better even without changing any limitations you may have.

The recording we linked to the previous post is a good example of a resilience prop. It can be helpful whether you are highly resilient at your core or not.

An experience Jerry had illustrates the power of these recordings:

“I was a full time professor for several decades. Contrary to a prevalent illusion, the days when academe provided a nice, relaxing job are long gone. I loved many aspects of my work, but in too many ways it was a “rat race”. I had started grammar school a year early, finished undergraduate school in less than four years, working nights the whole time, finished my doctoral program very quickly and then, after I became a professor, almost always had an extra job. My nickname in my Department was ‘The Busy Professor’.

I always had a dream of getting out of the rat race early just as I had started early. Our university began giving early retirement incentives, but I was not old enough to be eligible. Luckily another incentive was provided just as I reached an age that allowed me to grab it. I actually succeeded in getting out early!

Then, a month or two after my retirement, my bowels started producing copious amounts of blood.

I catastrophized. Just when I thought I was home safe, it appeared it all might well be taken away from me. I became intensely anxious and, even though I had worked in the areas of stress and resilience for many years, I was taken over by this anxiety.

Usually when something hits me hard like this, it takes a few days for me to remember that I know a lot about how to get a grip on it. Then I develop a resilience plan and start implementing it.

The plan I came up with included something to pull me out of the acute anxiety pretty quickly. I knew that a deep relaxation recording was very likely to at least take the edge off the anxiety.

I stretched out in a reasonably quiet comfortable spot, closed my eyes, and just passively listened. Soon I was unaware of the outside world.

When the exercise came to an end, it had produced far more than merely taking the edge off the anxiety. I was downright serene. Somehow, my entire perspective had shifted.

Later, the anxiety came back in a milder form, but I kept returning to the recording as I went through the days waiting for my colonoscopy.

The GI doctor found a suspicious polyp, so I had to wait another week or so for the results. I continued using the tape and was able to exert considerable control over the anxiety. Finally I learned that the polyp was not malignant. I have not had another one since.

Given how effective mere listening to a tape proved to be, why do we still think you should invest more effort to improve your inner, core resilience? For one thing, the extra effort can make you basically more resilient. As a result, you will tend to be consistently calmer, less reactive, happier, healthier and more effective. And there is more, which you will see in subsequent posts.
