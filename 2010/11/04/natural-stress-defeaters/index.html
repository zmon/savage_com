Natural Stress Defeaters

Posted on November 4, 2010 by Jerry Sheridan

…it is important that you clearly

  • identify the resilience procedures you want to try,
  • set up some sort of cuing system to make sure it comes to mind, and
  • make any needed arrangements so that you can implement the technique when you need to.

For years we have been collecting information about what ordinary people under stress do to sooth, calm, and comfort themselves. A particularly useful source was an article in the New York Times about how New Yorkers handle stress. The article was not terribly important for our purposes, but the author asked readers to write and describe their own approaches to recovering from their reactions to stress. Over 300 people responded. We added their comments to our previous collection and then categorized them into what appear to be meaningful dimensions.

The following are the categories we came up with, along with some examples:

Contact Comfort

With Pets

Curling up on the sofa with a cup of tea and my black cat.

My two cats are the best stress relief possible. When they’re upside down, tummy exposed looking totally relaxed, it’s hard to be too tense.

Having a small, neurotic dog whose only wish is to curl up next to you.

With People

Playing with my girlfriend.

Cuddling with my fiancé.

Having sex.

Getting a massage.

Asking for and accepting nurturing.

Pleasant, Engrossing Distraction

Shopping for something you like.

Sketching and Drawing.

Watching comedies.

Reading, especially something light and uplifting.

Getting out into nature.

Spending time with good friends.

Seeking out entertainment such as movies or shows.

Soothing Sensory Stimulation

Taking a hot bath or shower.

Listening to music.

Playing a musical instrument.

Eating pleasant treats, e.g. chocolate.

Exercise and Other Physical Activity

Swimming laps with goggles and earphones.

Engaging in sports you like.

Physical exercise, especially until really tired.

Going for long walks.

Jogging.

Working out at the gym.

Dancing.

Contemplative Relaxation

Meditating.

Practicing yoga.

Slow deep breathing.

Participating In Spiritual Activities

Praying.

Attending services.

Seeking spiritual guidance.

Read More

These may give you some good ideas for things to try using to recover from stress. Maybe some of them are things you’ve done in the past, but have allowed to slide away. That is easy to do since almost everyone is busy these days so there are many other activities to lure you away from doing what will help you bounce back.

Using methods like these is not as easy as it sounds, even if you know from experience that they work. When distressed we are, by definition, in a negative state of mind, and positive ideas tend not to occur to us. In addition, there may be obstacles to our putting practices into effect. I may know that I relax when listening to music, but if I fail to get recordings, keep the equipment in place, or arrange for a setting where I can listen to the music without bothering others, I may not be able to use even so simple a method.

So it is important that you clearly identify the resilience procedure you want to try, set up some sort of cuing system to make sure it comes to mind when you need it, and make any necessary arrangements so that you can implement the technique.

If you enjoy the procedure and you have done them many times, your brain will tend to give them high priority. They will have an advantage over less pleasant and less deeply engrained brain processes. Thus they are likely to shut down your distress reaction.

Try it, you’ll like it!
