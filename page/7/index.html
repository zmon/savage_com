Some Ways to Manage Your Mind and Therefore to Manage Your Body

Posted on November 14, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

I want to discuss life changing lessons I have learned from people who taught me how to train the mind in a way that produces improvements I and others want and maybe even desperately need.

The first of these people is Theodore Xenophon Barber, known by people who were connected to him as “Ted”.

Ted was famous for publishing many papers, I think in the hundreds of them, which are widely viewed as somehow refuting hypnosis. His studies repeatedly showed that people who were asked to pretend that they were hypnotized could perform what were generally regarded as remarkable feats due to hypnosis, even though they were merely told to act as if they were hypnotized.

For example, a demonstration of the powers of hypnosis was (and probably still is) to show that a small woman could, under hypnosis, carry the weight of a heavy guy who sat on her stomach when her head was on one chair and her feet on another. This was a common feat of stage hypnosis.

Ted showed that small people who were only told to pretend they were hypnotized could also do this kind of feat.

This is a good lesson about how much we underestimate our capabilities. I think we could honestly say that there is an “epidemic of self-underestimation”.

If you attempt to do this at home, it might or might not work. We DO massively underestimate our abilities, but we also have to activate them in the right way. For example, the person bearing the weight has to have her (or his) upper body arranged so that the shoulders, not the head are on one of the chairs.

We do not want to give more details that might encourage people to try this on their own. That is not a good idea. We only want to illustrate that there is a bit of finesse required for it to work. The results are remarkable, even taking the “finesse” into account.  The need to finesse the details illustrates the important point that, to use your mind effectively, you need training in the details. In this case, without being adequately trained, you might end up with a neck injury.

I suppose you can see why Ted was seen as a person who had refuted hypnosis. He was saying that most of us ordinary people can do what are regarded as extraordinary feats due to hypnosis.

Priming the Brain: Is the Research Valid?

Posted on November 2, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

We just finished our workshop, “From Negativity and Self-Criticism to Positivity and Self-Compassion: A Women’s Workshop”. We both enjoyed the experience. It was a great group.

We are minimizing emphasis on lectures (hard for me to do!), and focusing on using videos and our own audios to let people experience directly what we might otherwise talk about.

One topic included ways in which experiences we may not pay much attention to can influence how we behave, think, and experience subsequent events. In particular we introduced the group to “Priming” and “Emotional Contagion”.

Wikipedia provides a good technical definition of priming: Priming is a form of memory, technically called  implicit memory. Think of “how you ride a bike”. You just do it, and your conscious account of it might result in a crash. That is the sense in which it is implicit.

With priming, exposure to one stimulus influences a response to a later stimulus and conscious awareness of that influence is irrelevant.

A good example of priming, which I cited during the workshop, is a study in which people who were exposed to words that primed them to think of old age, e.g. Florida, Social Security, and the like, led them to walk more slowly and with a postural stoop linked to stereotypes of old age.

There are many studies on priming that have yielded similar results. For example, one study reported that people who were given cold versus warm drinks to hold tended to react to people subsequently encountered as warmer or colder, depending on whether they had held the warm or cold drink.

This is a useful effect for those of us who want to function better in the world. It implies that arranging our environment the right way, even in ways that our conscious minds might rate as irrelevant, can make us function better.

We got this information from a very nice lecture by Shawn Achor which was used as part of “Pledge Week” on Public TeIevision. We should have done a careful review of the original research and any follow ups. Unfortunately we were working so hard on designing our workshop that we didn’t even think of the possibility that there might be something wrong with the research we were citing.

Finding a Path to a More Resilient Life; the Devil is in the Details.

Posted on October 17, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

In our last post we pointed out that there are “quirks” of the human mind that limit us, but also, if they are well used, can help us to be more resilient. Earlier, we had pointed out that the human mind, granted its remarkable powers, has surprisingly sharp limits on the amount of information it can process at any one time.

Our brains have very limited “channel capacity” or “bandwidth”. They can easily run out of resources for processing new information.

The silver lining of these limitations is that, if we focus on positives, we limit the space for negatives, and thus improve our critical ratio of positives to negatives. So the limitation can be turned into an advantage by filling our narrow brain “channel” with positives.

What else can we do to increase our positivity?

In this post we will explain some small shifts in how we imagine what we will gain by making changes in our lives that can help us to be more resilient (or, in fact, to change any of our habits).

Our Next Step in Resilience Training

Posted on September 20, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

Debbie and I have been devoting all our available time to redesigning our resilience training program with changes that are much more dramatic, and thus time-consuming, than we expected. We are incorporating new scientific findings, but in many ways the new design takes us back to the roots of our interest in resilience, which reach deeply not only into scientific findings but also are anchored in our years of clinical and training experience.

The main changes will be no surprise to regular readers of this blog. They will have followed us in our enthusiastic incorporation of methods for tapping into the emotional parts of the brain to augment mere shifting of cognitions (self-talk). Our limbic, emotional brains must change for us to feel truly self-soothed, safe as well as warm and caring toward ourselves.

We have already presaged these shifts in recent posts, but we will have much more to say about them in the future. The changes are good for both the trainees and us, since they are more effective and they also allow us to make more use of our clinical and training experience.

Try to Remove or Change Your Sources of Stress

Posted on September 6, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

Normally onresilience.com is about ways you can become more resistant to stressful demands. That was not always our main focus. There was a time when we placed much more emphasis on changing social and physical environments.

Consider the “Demand-Control” model of work stress. Its key idea is that when your work makes high demands, but gives you little control over how you do the work, that is a formula for breakdown, e.g. increased cardiovascular disease.

There is an abundance of evidence that empowering employees is good for both organizations and employees. Yet we shifted our direction toward teaching people how to keep themselves well even when they were overloaded and disempowered. Why did we do that?

Because, at least in our part of the world, powerful forces were stripping most people of the modest control they had. We saw the writing on the wall, and wanted to make the best of the new realities we faced. So we shifted our focus to the best ways to flourish in spite of unsupportive environments.

However, we do not want to go too far with the shift, so today’s post will be on taking a look at things in your day to day world that distress you, and modifying them as much as you can.

Reducing Negativity in Your Mind and Your Life

Posted on August 2, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

To improve our positivity/negativity ratio, positives have to increase and negatives have to decrease. Fortunately, a limitation inherent in our brains leads to an automatic reduction of negatives as we increase our positives.

Our brains have enormous capacity for memory, and it’s not easy for even the most powerful computers to do better than us. But, at any moment in time, we can process only a very small amount of information.

The more we load our minds with positive experiences, the less room there will be for negatives.

Still that is not likely to be enough; we also need to target our negative thoughts and experiences directly.

In several previous posts we have talked about how to deal with negative thinking, especially self-criticism. Those posts 1, 2, 3, 4, are a good guide to dealing with our negative thoughts.

But improving our patterns of thought is not the same as improving our life situation. That requires coming up with a path that will lead to more positive experiences.

We have to take a number of steps to find and walk such a path. An obvious step is finding the path, but we also need to develop a plan of action, be assertive, etc. Taking a problem solving attitude is a pervasive influence on helping us through those steps.

Having a problem-solving attitude toward reducing the impact of negative experiences is an important component of resilience.
