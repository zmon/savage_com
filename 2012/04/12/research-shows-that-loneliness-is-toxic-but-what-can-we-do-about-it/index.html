Research Shows that Loneliness is Toxic, but What Can We Do About It?

Posted on April 12, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

Loneliness can be toxic, and even lethal.  Among other things, it can shorten your life, and make it more likely you will suffer from our leading killers, cardiovascular diseases.

Most of us are lonely at times, but it gets risky when loneliness hangs on for a long time, when it is “chronic”.

Common sense would seem to tell us that social support is an antidote to loneliness, but life isn’t that simple. A LOT of research shows that we can live healthier and longer lives with the support of others, but it also shows that mere lists of how many sources of support we have are not good at predicting our health or how long we live.  How we think about our lives and ourselves as well as how we view and mentally process the attempts of others to support us is crucial.

For example, our degree of self-compassion can determine how much we can take in and profit from social support. We have to be kind to ourselves to take in and assimilate the kindness of others.

If we tell ourselves that we are “no good” or “rotten” or “wasted time”, it will be hard to profit from someone’s attempts to care for us. It is reminiscent of the old quote from Groucho Marx “I don’t care to belong to any club that will have me as a member”.

With attitudes like that, if someone holds us in high esteem, we are probably going to dismiss them. When we do that, we push away the very help we need, and also unwittingly demean those who try to help us.

We have been told that we should love others as we love ourselves, but a crucial step needed to make this really work is to learn to know and love ourselves. Not to advance some narrow, perhaps illusory achievement that seems at the moment important to us, but to actually learn to understand and care for ourselves, and then to give back that kind of caring to others.

If we can learn to love ourselves (really to improve on our ability to understand what is deep in our hearts and to actually allow ourselves to care about it), then we have a chance to include others in this real understanding and real caring.

So it is clear that there are some things that we have to add to what our friends contribute to the support we need.

It helps to make at least some small improvement in how much we realize and accept that it is very hard to be a decent human being, and to care for and sooth ourselves in spite of our failings.

Think of how often people speak critically about other people, even about their closest friends. It doesn’t mean that people are terrible. It means that we are all human beings trying to live good lives, and inevitably falling short of the ideal.

Once we grasp that we are all human and that it is in the very nature of humanity that none of us can get it quite right, it becomes easier to accept ourselves and to accept others as worthwhile, though flawed. Then we can really take the support we are offered and fully make use of it, and be grateful to those who are caring and kind enough to proffer it.
