The Love and Support You Get From Others Enhances Your Health and Well-Being.

Posted on February 29, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

So far our posts have emphasized building your inner resilience. In particular, we have emphasized cultivating positive attitudes, practicing skills that help you minimize your stress reactions, and, learning to be compassionate with yourself. All of these are things you can do by yourself.

But resilience is not all inside you; you are not an island. The people you are connected to are centrally important to your resilience. There is much scientific evidence that your health and well-being are strongly influenced by the people who are linked to you.

A recent example came to our attention and triggered this post. It showed clearly that better work performance and success depended on whether people had strong emotional support at home.

In a recent study by Wayne Hochwarter and his colleagues at Florida State University, emotional support at home was linked to:

  • A 50 percent greater satisfaction with marriage.
  • A 33 percent greater likelihood of having better relationships with co-workers.
  • A 25 percent greater likelihood of being able to concentrate at work and a 25 percent lower chance of feeling fatigued at work and at home.
  • A 30 percent likelihood of less guilt associated with home/family neglect.
  • A 30 percent lower likelihood of being critical of others (spouse, children) at home.

People who have emotional support are clearly at an advantage, and Dr. Hochwarter’s work gives is just one tiny bit of the large body of evidence for that.

There are also personal skills that help you make the most of the emotional support you have. You can help your supporters support you. Often the people who are available for support do not really know what you need. A good example of this from our experience is that women seeking support from their husbands may not want to be told what to do. More often they want somebody to listen to them and be on their side. Husbands tend to think that supporting their wives means telling them how they should handle the situation.

What is our “take home” message?

  • Support from others is very important to many aspects of your well-being. Friends and family are also a major resilience resource.
  • If you are not getting the support you need, you may be able to coach potential supporters on how to do better.
