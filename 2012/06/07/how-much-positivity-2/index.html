How Much Positivity?

Posted on June 7, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

Many scientifically based publications refer to a ratio of positive to negative experiences that you have to reach to live a happy, healthy, and successful life. Is there really such a critical ratio that is scientifically valid?

We think there is.

We will describe several studies that have confirmed it. In a subsequent post, we plan to focus on some simple ways to improve your positivity to negativity ratio.

The concept of the critical ratio was explored and developed by Marcial Losada and his colleagues. He and his team meticulously studied 60 business teams in planning sessions, with observers recording everything that was said. There were three dimensions of interest

  1. How positive or negative participants were.
  2. Whether comments were self-focused or other-focused.
  3. The degree to which statements were based on inquiry (asking questions) or advocacy (defending a point of view).

The businesses were divided into three groups, based on the degree of positivity displayed in their meetings. He then assessed the degree to which the businesses were flourishing or stagnating. His criteria of flourishing included profitability, customer satisfaction, and ratings by superiors, peers, and subordinates.

Businesses in the top 25% were clearly flourishing. In contrast, those in the bottom 30% were stagnating or failing.

Losada developed a mathematical model of the relationship between the positivity/negativity ratio and business success. The model indicated that the “tipping point” was about 3 positives for each negative.

But does the 3 to 1 ratio apply generally or is it specific to business planning and business success?

Losada discovered Barbara Frederickson’s “broaden and build” model of the effects of positivity. She has shown that, with positivity, people widen their perspective whereas those who are high in negativity tend to narrow their field of consciousness, and thus to lack creativity in solving problems.

Losada learned about Barbara Frederickson’s research on the effects of positivity, he wrote her to ask whether she would be interested in writing a joint paper that synthesized their work, she had just had a baby, so didn’t respond for a while, but fortunately, he persisted, and they eventually got together for what turned out to be a very exciting and creative meeting.

The next question was whether her own research findings did or did not confirm the prediction of his mathematical model, and the meeting was enough to lead her to devote her research time to exploring this possibility. (For academics, shifting the focus of their work is a really big deal; it is at the very core of their life commitments and their performance in their jobs).

Well it turned out that Frederickson’s data also indicated that a ratio of about 3 to 1 predicted whether people were flourishing or languishing in their lives. This work is all summarized in non-technical language in Dr. Fredrickson’s book “Positivity”

The 3 to 1 ratio is the crucial point where the power of positivity in controlling the effects of negative becomes clear. Losada has said that he has seen ratios at high as 5.6 to 1. So positives anywhere from 3 times negatives to roughly twice that have been shown to result in success and well-being.

John Gottman, an eminent researcher on factors that make marriages succeed or fail, also found a ratio within that range that was related to marital success (5 to 1). If you are interested in successful marriage, (or, no doubt, successful relationships in general, you should take a minute to view this video of Gottman explaining in more detail, the ratio’s practical significance.

 

 

We are talking about things that are very important to most of us, job success and marital success, and a lot more, so it would be wise for all of us to learn how to increase our positivity and reduce our negativity so we at least reach that 3 to one ratio.

Sounds like something very difficult to do? This is partly because most of us have many positive things in our lives, but give scant attention to them. Or positive things we could easily do, but fail to take action. Negatives tend to be more memorable and when the big ones hit us, it is hard to keep our attention on the many positives we have or can easily call up.
