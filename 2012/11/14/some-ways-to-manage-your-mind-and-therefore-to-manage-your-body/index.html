Some Ways to Manage Your Mind and Therefore to Manage Your Body

Posted on November 14, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

I want to discuss life changing lessons I have learned from people who taught me how to train the mind in a way that produces improvements I and others want and maybe even desperately need.

The first of these people is Theodore Xenophon Barber, known by people who were connected to him as “Ted”.

Ted was famous for publishing many papers, I think in the hundreds of them, which are widely viewed as somehow refuting hypnosis. His studies repeatedly showed that people who were asked to pretend that they were hypnotized could perform what were generally regarded as remarkable feats due to hypnosis, even though they were merely told to act as if they were hypnotized.

For example, a demonstration of the powers of hypnosis was (and probably still is) to show that a small woman could, under hypnosis, carry the weight of a heavy guy who sat on her stomach when her head was on one chair and her feet on another. This was a common feat of stage hypnosis.

Ted showed that small people who were only told to pretend they were hypnotized could also do this kind of feat.

This is a good lesson about how much we underestimate our capabilities. I think we could honestly say that there is an “epidemic of self-underestimation”.

If you attempt to do this at home, it might or might not work. We DO massively underestimate our abilities, but we also have to activate them in the right way. For example, the person bearing the weight has to have her (or his) upper body arranged so that the shoulders, not the head are on one of the chairs.

We do not want to give more details that might encourage people to try this on their own. That is not a good idea. We only want to illustrate that there is a bit of finesse required for it to work. The results are remarkable, even taking the “finesse” into account.  The need to finesse the details illustrates the important point that, to use your mind effectively, you need training in the details. In this case, without being adequately trained, you might end up with a neck injury.

I suppose you can see why Ted was seen as a person who had refuted hypnosis. He was saying that most of us ordinary people can do what are regarded as extraordinary feats due to hypnosis.

When I first met Ted, I had already read a many of his books and papers; that is why I worked to arrange for him to give some lectures and training for the faculty and students at our Department of Psychology. Since I was the faculty member who had arranged for him to come, I was charged with picking him up at the airport.

On the way into town, he asked me what kind of work I do.

I said, “I am teaching people how to use mental imagery to help them maneuver through life”.  He responded, “Oh, you are also using hypnosis.”  I responded, “No, I just use things like guided imagery”.

We went through several rounds of this, and he finally said, “You will understand why I am calling what you do “hypnosis” after you attend my workshop.”

He was right. I now see that we are all hypnotized all of the time. All of us live in a “cultural trance” that is affirmed and strengthened by others in our culture.

His research had not refuted hypnosis; it had shown that what has been labeled “hypnosis” is an ordinary way that we all guide our minds, whether effectively or ineffectively. Many people guide their minds to make their lives miserable and ruin their health. That same mental power could as well make them happy and healthy.

In Ted’s training session he gave several demonstrations. For example, he asked us to rest an elbow on the desk in front of us (It was a college classroom), and then guided us into imagining that we had received an anesthetic injection. He asked to think and imagine with him as he suggested, step by step, what would happen if we had actually been anesthetized. The large majority of people in the audience experienced what he suggested.

We can call this “hypnosis”, but it is a natural ability we all have and use all the time. We can suggest to ourselves that “Life sucks”, that we are “useless”, or what have you. On the other hand we can think and imagine in ways that guide us to feeling that life is good and that we can make it better and better.

I think I first got a sense of what Ted’s research had taught him when I took him to lunch before we went to the University. At one point the waitress asked if we wanted anything else. He answered her with a look and tone that struck me as that of an enthusiastic child and said “Can I have some more milk?”

He had taught himself to get real joy out of simple things in life.

Later, in the training workshop, he talked about working with people who wanted to quit smoking. At first he did something that is common in our “cultural trance”, being negative and punitive.

He had his clients think and imagine the horrible consequences of smoking. He described taking a man step by step through imagining that he had a cough that didn’t go away, that he went to a doctor who, after some tests, told him he had lung cancer. He had him imagine the subsequent steps all the way to the client’s being in a coffin lowered into his grave as his young daughter sobbed and  said, “Daddy, if only you had quit smoking!”.

Well, that did not work.

Then he described what DID work. He suggested that people who were having trouble giving up cigarettes were depressed and did not know how to savor simple pleasures in life. Then he had them imagine and think about enjoying various aspects of life. One of them was the pleasure of drinking a beverage that they really liked.

Hearing him mention the example of enjoying a beverage, I thought of the way he asked for “more milk”. Then I knew he was telling us how he had learned to live an exuberant life.

Later, I will tell you some details of what Ted had discovered about effective ways to guide the mind in ways that work for you.

(Edited by Debbie S. Sheridan)
