Priming the Brain: Is the Research Valid?

Posted on November 2, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

We just finished our workshop, “From Negativity and Self-Criticism to Positivity and Self-Compassion: A Women’s Workshop”. We both enjoyed the experience. It was a great group.

We are minimizing emphasis on lectures (hard for me to do!), and focusing on using videos and our own audios to let people experience directly what we might otherwise talk about.

One topic included ways in which experiences we may not pay much attention to can influence how we behave, think, and experience subsequent events. In particular we introduced the group to “Priming” and “Emotional Contagion”.

Wikipedia provides a good technical definition of priming: Priming is a form of memory, technically called  implicit memory. Think of “how you ride a bike”. You just do it, and your conscious account of it might result in a crash. That is the sense in which it is implicit.

With priming, exposure to one stimulus influences a response to a later stimulus and conscious awareness of that influence is irrelevant.

A good example of priming, which I cited during the workshop, is a study in which people who were exposed to words that primed them to think of old age, e.g. Florida, Social Security, and the like, led them to walk more slowly and with a postural stoop linked to stereotypes of old age.

There are many studies on priming that have yielded similar results. For example, one study reported that people who were given cold versus warm drinks to hold tended to react to people subsequently encountered as warmer or colder, depending on whether they had held the warm or cold drink.

This is a useful effect for those of us who want to function better in the world. It implies that arranging our environment the right way, even in ways that our conscious minds might rate as irrelevant, can make us function better.

We got this information from a very nice lecture by Shawn Achor which was used as part of “Pledge Week” on Public TeIevision. We should have done a careful review of the original research and any follow ups. Unfortunately we were working so hard on designing our workshop that we didn’t even think of the possibility that there might be something wrong with the research we were citing.

Well, it turns out that at least one follow-up study indicated that the results depended on what the experimenters expected to happen. Here is a very good account of the critical research.

Now for me to miss that is pretty close to inexcusable. I have been ranting about the importance of controlling for “experimenter effects” for decades in books that I wrote. It’s a topic that comes up on many Saturdays when I do my walks in the woods with a dear friend. We talk about his field, Political Science, and mine, which I think you know about.

What are experimenter effects? They are ways in which biases and expectations of experimenters can lead to the results they expect. This is not deliberate fraud, but “unconscious” influence.

As it happens, I had a close relationship with a person who, as an undergraduate taking a course in Experimental Psychology, was duped into believing she had been assigned a “dumb” rat to train in a “Skinner box”. The idea was to train the rats to respond appropriately by pressing a bar that could lead to rewards.

The rats were, in fact, randomly selected. There was no reason to think they were dumber or smarter than others. But half of them were tagged as dumb, and the other half didn’t get that negative stereotype.

I remember how annoyed my friend was at being stuck with a “dumb” rat- just couldn’t teach it anything! The rat behaved in accord with its stereotype as dumb.

Over and over I have complained to my hiking friend about how the importance of experimenter effects has been forgotten. Yet, in this case, I forgot about it.

Which is a good lesson in how easily even those of us who know better can be taken over by social influences.

I suppose the best I can say is “Watch out and stay critical” when you read about results of experiments. Probably a good rule is to try out any new ways for attaining your goals that are backed by credible research, give them a fair chance to succeed or fail, then decide whether the principles work for you.

Published research should be viewed with caution. There are many incentives to report interesting findings, and few incentives to rain on the parade.

A word of caution: the person who published the study now called into question is arguing back. Our warning does not assume that the original finding is wrong. We will stay alert and see how it comes out.
