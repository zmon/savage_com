Does Our DNA Have Final Say Over Our Reactions to Stress?

Posted on January 17, 2012 by Jerry Sheridan

When people ask whether resilience is genetic, I tend to suspect they are asking whether they can reasonably hope to improve how they act to stress. I worry that they will give up without a fight because they believe they are forever closed in a prison of misery by the chemical code of their genes.

Is the way we react inborn? Is it unchangeable?

This video by Dr. Dean Ornish answers these questions well and concisely. His emphasis on factors that influence gene expression is right on target. Gene expression is strongly influenced by lifestyle and skills of managing stress. There is a whole field, called “epigenetics” devoted to the ways in which DNA is “expressed” by various triggers, many of which are environmental.

Here is what I understand about genes and resilience from years, really decades, of studying both resilience and the biological bases of behavior.

It seems obvious to me that part of our reaction to stress is influenced by genetic factors. Decades ago I was asked to present my research at a conference organized by the U.S. Veterans Administration. I don’t really remember what I presented, but I clearly remember what a man from Arkansas, Oddist Murphree presented on the genetics of vulnerability.

Murphree had interbred tense and anxious dogs. Generations of these dog’s offspring were extremely tense and anxious. (He wasn’t just a mad scientist. He wanted to find ways to help people who suffer from anxiety and tension by using nervous dogs as research models for nervous humans.)

I recall his showing a slide of a dog so tense that, even when his usual caretaker pushed down on his nose, his hind legs went upward, as if he were made of wood.

Murphree’s research was enough to make me strongly inclined to believe that there is an important genetic component that determines how we react, or over-react to stress.

Most people think that, if something is genetic, it cannot be changed. Actually whether the gene gets turned on or not is influenced by many factors, as Dr. Ornish points out.

Being “hard-wired” does not mean that a trait cannot be modified. Commonly we are born with a general template of what we might be, but our environment and what we do with it has to fill in the details of that template.

Bird song provides a good example of a modifiable genetic template. A given species of bird has a commonly shared set of songs that they sing. But the young ones sing the equivalent of human “baby talk”. The rest is fleshed out by the songs they hear in their environment. So birds of the same species from different parts of the world may have different “accents”.

Thus our genetic code can provide us a beginning template that provides a platform for developing a final pattern that is well adapted to the specifics or our environment.

Furthermore, it is well known that our brains tend to follow a pattern of “use it or lose it”. If we do not use brain circuits, they tend to deteriorate and stop functioning. Neuroscientists have shown, for example that, at birth, kittens have cells in their visual cortex that react to useful aspects of their visual environment. If the kittens are kept from seeing that environment, those cells quit functioning. Neuroscientists now place emphasis on “neuroplasticity”, the ability of our brains to change in response to environments or other factors.

We can play the DNA hand we have been dealt poorly or play it well. We can thrive and succeed in spite of what our DNA stipulates. Doing the best with the DNA we inherit is a central point of resilience training. Even when DNA and environment conspire to make things difficult, many people find ways to do well.

So I give genes their due, but, I also know that we can learn to respond effectively and do well despite our vulnerability. Not to be super-human, but to respond effectively and do well. That’s what resilience training is all about.
