Anticipating Barriers as an Aspect of Positivity.

Posted on January 7, 2015 by Jerry and Debbie Sheridan

Should you do all in your power to block every negative thought from coming into your mind? Some people have argued that you cannot afford the luxury of a negative thought.

A common refrain in our society is “Be positive!” And there is a great deal of research supporting the idea that positivity is good for you. But can you really turn your mind from negative to positive on demand? Does that really help?

Convincing research indicates that pretending we are not distressed when we really are results in physiological responses that are known to be unhealthy, e.g. high blood pressure. This doesn’t happen if we get to think and talk about our feelings of distress, which inevitably involves letting negative thoughts and feelings enter your mind.

More than a few highly competent researchers have argued that there are actually benefits to negativity.

One of them, Dr. Julie Norem, maintains that many of us do well by using a strategy of “defensive pessimism”. This means that, when we set a goal, we go on to review all the things that might go wrong, then develop ways of preventing the anticipated problems from blocking our success.

To us, that strategy is just common sense, but we would not have thought of it as pessimism.

In the famous Harvard Grant Study, participants were tested and interviewed regularly over their lifetime.  Anticipation, a tendency to look ahead for problems and avoid being blind-sided by them was identified as a healthy way to live. Anticipation is one of the “healthy defense mechanisms” identified in that study. And it seems to be “defensive pessimism” by another name.

It’s not clear to us why that kind of behavior should be categorized as negative and labeled “defensive pessimism”. It could as well be deemed a way to deal with reality and prepare for problems we are likely to confront. It is more like “realism” than “pessimism”.

It also seems to us that defensive pessimism implies that you are hopeful enough to look for solutions that can get you to your goal. That’s a pretty positive attitude, very different from something like “It’s no use. There is nothing I can do to succeed in this situation”, which is clearly negative. See this earlier post for an interesting case of shifting a person from hopeless pessimism to problem solving.

In our research on resilience, we found that optimism is linked to a “problem solving attitude”, which seems to be exactly what defensive pessimists have.

However, it doesn’t matter what you call it; anticipating problems and finding solutions to them is a good way to deal effectivly with life’s challenges.
