Don’t Let Overwhelming Distress Take You Down.

Posted on December 14, 2011 by Jerry Sheridan

When one distressing thing happens after another, it’s natural to be overwhelmed. Though it is natural, that doesn’t mean it is inevitable. There are a few things you can do to keep these stressful things from piling up, but many, even most of them are out of your control. However, you CAN have control over your reactions to them. In this post we outline ways to reign in your reactions to stress and avoid becoming overwhelmed. We include links that provide more detail about

Here are the steps to take:

Before the stress becomes overwhelming.

If you are not already overwhelmed, you should prepare by learning some basic skills to neutralize distress.

  1. Breath Control: Learn how to give yourself immediate relief by coaching yourself in the use of tactical breathing. Breath control is a central tool we have to minimize how our bodies respond to stress.
    1. Practice breathing all the way down to your abdomen. If your belly doesn’t move out as you breathe in, you are not getting full breaths.
    2. Besides breathing deeply, train yourself to extend the exhalation phase of the breathing cycle. An easy way to do this is by mentally saying the word “relax” in any extended way “reeeeelaaaax” as you breathe out. That way you get more effective breathing and also, by using the word “relax”, suggest to your mind that it shift to a relaxed state.
  2. Self-Soothing: Train yourself to do what soothes and calms you and start it at the first signs of stress. Look at the link and identify some for you to practice for calming and self-soothing. When something riles you, think of it as an opportunity to practice resilience skills.
  3. Listen to soothing, relaxing audios (which is a method we, ourselves, routinely use when the going gets tough).  There is a free audio on this blog that works well, but use whatever works for you.
  4. Self-Talk: Remind yourself that you have survived other harsh situations, and so you have reason to believe you can handle this as well.
    1. Talk to yourself in calming ways. Imagine how an ideal parent who loves you would talk to you and talk to yourself that way.
    2. Have compassion for yourself; it only makes things worse to criticize yourself when under stress. You need someone on your side, and you should definitely be on your own side.
    3. Don’t know how to be caring and support yourself?  Well, try “acting”; maybe you aren’t ready for Broadway, but you can probably manage this. The more you do it, the more natural it will become.
  5. Social Support: If you are overwhelmed or close to it, your mind is probably overloaded, so you need outside help. Look for people who can help you to stay in control when you are becoming overwhelmed. Call on them when you are distressed, talk things out, and ask for help when you need it.
    1. If you haven’t established a base of supporters, start right now to develop one. Be generous in giving others support so they feel okay investing their energy in supporting you.
    2. Encourage and teach your supporters to have a clear sense of how to meet your needs. Supporters need to be told what kind of support you need. Many people end up disappointed with the support they get, but they haven’t really asked for what they need.
    3. When you are distressed, it is often tempting to direct your anger and frustration toward people who would like to support you. Do not discourage your supporters. If possible, be their cheerleader and their coach. You will probably get a good return on your investment if you do that.
  6. Use Your Imagination: If you don’t get the needed support from others, imagine yourself in supportive situations
    1. Picture and ponder scenes in which you have felt good and at peace. “Ponder” means that you stay with these imaginings for a while, long enough to let them have an effect on both your thoughts and your feelings.
    2. Imagine yourself with a person, real or imaginary, who really cares about you and backs you up.
    3. If you can’t imagine, or feel uncomfortable imagining a supportive person, try imagining a caring pet who loves you unconditionally.
    4. If you can’t imagine a supportive animal, imagine yourself sitting in a park resting your back against a tree. Take in the feeling that the earth supports you and the tree has your back.
  7. Exercise: Take a brisk walk; it can calm and energize you. A brisk walk is enough; you do not have to prepare for a marathon. Years ago Dr. Herbert DeVries did a study showing that a 15 minute brisk walk resulted in greater relaxation than valium. If  you can devote more time and energy to the exercise, you will probably get more relief up to a point. You probably already know the routine cautions about checking with a physician before you start a new exercise program. This is just in case there are medical reasons to limit exertion. Most of us can do modest amounts of exercise without any significant risks of nasty side effects, but it’s risky to make big changes in our level of exertion without consulting with someone who knows our true physical state.
