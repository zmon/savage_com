Resilience Is More than Bouncing Back From Stress

Posted on July 5, 2011 by Jerry Sheridan

In this post I want to take a closer look at what resilience is and who can gain from training to increase it.

People often tell me that I should explain resilience as the ability to “bounce back” from stress. I hate that view of resilience. It always reminds me of those inflated dummies that are designed to come right back to an upright position after they are punched, setting themselves up to be punched again. That is not resilience. It is mindlessness, literally in this case.

A much better description of resilience is “playing a poor hand well”. There is a pretty good book by that title, and I commend the author for the book and the title.

But even that title fails to get it quite right. Resilience is having the ability to play any hand well. If you were dealt a poor hand, resilience is the set of abilities that enable you to perform above expectations. If you were dealt a good hand, it is the set of abilities that enable you to make the most of it. In other words, resilience enables you to deal with stress efficiently and effectively, to recover from stress more quickly and more completely. But, stress aside, it enables you to perform at your best level, whether in your work, in sports, or in relationships.

So it is not easy to target precisely who needs resilience training because the audience is really just about everybody. Maybe the best way to pinpoint who is a good match for training and who is not is to break people down into:

  • Those who need help in developing their skills for dealing with stress and living up to their potential in life and also know they need help.
  • Those who need help in developing those skills and do not know it.

A final word.

Years ago I used to lend a large set of my data on stress and resilience for use in an advanced statistics class, where the students were learning to find the meanings hidden in masses of data. The instructor always invited me to sit in on a long session in which each student reported on their findings. In one of those classes a student who was a real standout in statistics reported that she had detected a very large factor in the data, for which she had struggled to give an adequate name. She decided to call it “Good Life”.

It was the factor that made the largest contribution to resilience. After many more years of study, we identified that factor as “resilient attitudes and skills”. I often think that “Good Life” gives an excellent sense of what developing resilient attitudes and skills might provide.
