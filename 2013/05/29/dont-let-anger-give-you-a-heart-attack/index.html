Don’t Let Anger Give You a Heart Attack.

Posted on May 29, 2013 by Jerry and Debbie Sheridan

What should we do with anger bottled up inside of us? A recent article in the Washington Post discussed a serious effect of one option, that of letting out the raw anger, even to the point of throwing things and threatening to hurt other people.

A common theme for some mental health professionals has been that we should get the anger out. For these therapists, sessions can consist of banging a pillow (usually representing  parents who were taken as having failed the client), with a bat that has many of the properties of a nasty club, but is in fact safely padded.

The Washington Post article describes a study in which about 4000 people who had heart attacks were asked about their experiences of anger. Heart attacks were twice as likely to occur after an episode of anger. There was a tendency for the heart attacks to occur within two hours of the anger episode.

Patients were also asked to rate how intense their anger episode had been, and the more intense the anger episode, the more likely patients were to have a heart attack.

So we have therapists trying to help us get the anger out versus research showing that expressing anger can lead to a heart attack.  So what are we to do? Should we bottled up our anger or express it?

The Washington Post article says that “Bottling up of emotions is thought to harm both mind and body, but a new study suggests that doing the opposite may be no better.” What I want to do here is to be sure readers don’t take the options as limited to either “bottling up” or “doing the opposite”, blowing your stack.

There is a lot of research indicating that, even though letting out raw anger harms you, striving to get clear about, articulate, and express your emotions has positive effects on the mind and body, not to mention on social relationships.

This is very different from blowing your stack or “venting”, which has long been known to be an ineffective and typically damaging way to deal with distressing experiences.

It is important to learn how to understand your feelings when distressed, and to articulate them without unnecessarily alienating others, especially others who might be able to help you minimize any potential damage coming from your stress. “Blowing your stack” is almost certain to amplify the impact of stress.

With stress amplification, your response to stress increases the physiological responses that make heart attacks more likely. That kind of response also may alienate your potential support systems and thus make   you less resilient and more vulnerable. It’s amazing how many people follow this totally ineffective pattern of stress management.

Your anger is there to protect you. It helps you to push against obstacles that can block you from reaching your goals, including people who are trying to dominate you in order to reach their own goals.

If you use anger well, it will be a powerful source of energy to help you reach your goals.

If, instead, you amplify the impact of your stress, you become your own enemy, often your own worst enemy.

To deal with anger you should not act on it. Don’t let your brain’s motor system rule you. You should attend to the anger and find out what it means.

To do this, you have to open a mental space in which you can gain a deep understanding of why you are angry.

That way you are more likely to move toward your sensible goals instead of undermining yourself.

You should take time to attend to your angry reactions, focusing first on what you feel in your body.  Visit with those bodily reactions until they can be expressed in words.

When you do that, you shift from unwise, primitive commands from the parts of your brain that lead to mindless action. Instead you pull together and synthesize thoughts with emotions and then take action.

That synthesis is most likely to be the creative source of actions that are both wise and effective.
