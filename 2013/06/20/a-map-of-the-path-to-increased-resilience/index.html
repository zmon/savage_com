A Map of the Path to Increased Resilience.

Posted on June 20, 2013 by Jerry and Debbie Sheridan

Underlying our posts is a three-factor model of resilience which was derived from research done as part of my role as a professor. Our posts tend to be on specific attitudes or actions that are statistically confirmed aspects of one of the three factors. That is not because we select them only if they match our model. Our model just matches the data.

The blog was not started to publicize or support the model. It started as an outlet to keep me from constantly adding new findings to a book Debbie and I wrote to teach people how to improve their resilience.

To identify the core factors that underlie resilience we used a method called factor analysis. Done correctly, it can reveal the deep structure of whatever you are studying. So, for example, if you used it on three dimensional objects, like boxes, it would tell you that there are three dimensions, height, width and length.

We determined the dimensions of resilience and found that there were three. We also replicated that finding in two additional studies, and both of them confirmed the three dimensions.

To explain the factors here would be redundant, since we have already done that in this post.

For now, we will discuss the number one factor that improves resilience.

What good is this to you?

Knowing the core dimensions will help you pinpoint all you can do to improve your resilience.

The number one influence on resilience is developing resilience-promoting  attitudes and skills.  These account for so much of resilience that, for a while, I was tempted to think of Factor One alone as just plain resilience, and ignore the other two factors. But too much evidence showed that those factors can be crucial for many people.

So what are the attitudes and skills you should try to develop?

  • Being optimistic and hopeful instead of pessimistic and hopeless.
  • Seeing distressing or painful life events as problems to be solved instead of things to be lamented and cursed.
  • Looking for and maybe even finding meaning in what we do and what we experience instead of just slogging through life.
  • Learning how to sooth, calm and replenish yourself, and then applying what you have learned to your everyday life when you are distressed, and also when you are feeling okay. (The latter is important in order to develop habits of resilience.).

Some of the attitudes under factor one that help us be more resilient are

  1. Being optimistic and hopeful versus viewing life with pessimism or despair.

Here are posts that tell you how to do that:

Increasing your optimism.

Decreasing your negativity.

  1. Viewing our lives, our choices, and our actions as meaningful versus seeing them as meaningless.

Here is a post to help you do that.

Finding  meaning.

  1. Taking a problem-solving attitude toward stressful situations and anticipating predictable complications.

Here is a post that shows you how to do that:

Problem-Solving Attitude.

  1. Learning and applying  the skills that sooth and replenish you instead of taking actions that make things worse.

There are many posts that show how to do that:

Training yourself in mindfulness.

Breath control for resilience.

Learning deep relaxation.
