Hazards of a Life That is All Work and No Play.

Posted on January 9, 2013 by Jerry Sheridan

For some time now, it has been obvious that Secretary of State, Hillary Clinton, was exhausted. She has coped with many extreme stresses for years. Recently she collapsed and suffered a concussion. Then she had a blood clot near her brain. She has recovered, and we do not need to tell her she needs to lighten up her life. Even before her collapse she had made it clear that she would no longer be U.S. Secretary of State.

It is a shame that so many people work so hard that they damage themselves. I wonder how many other people out there are pushing themselves to such damaging levels?  Why do we not refuse to continue when stress has obviously reached those risky levels?

There are many good reasons to demand so much of ourselves.

Many people have to do it just to make ends meet.  Others may push themselves out of personal ambition. Many sacrifice their personal well-being on behalf of people they care about, ranging from friends and family to fellow citizens.

When we choose to make such sacrifices, it is important to be aware of the risks to our health, and even our lives.

The stressful “all work and no play” way of living can be deadly. For example, cardiologist C. Noel Bairey Merz concluded, after reviewing the research literature on psychosocial stress and various endpoints of cardiovascular disease (CVD) that “…psychosocial stress contributes to CVD in multiple ways and may well be the decisive factor determining survival or death in the majority of cases.”

There is an important review of scientific research on the relationship between the length of our lives and such things as joy, cheerfulness, and happiness. When Drs. Ed Diener and Micaela Chan systematically reviewed research on the relationship between health, longevity and a positive attitude toward your life, the astonishing results were that living a happy life predicts how long we live better than whether we have risky habits like smoking. And the differences in life span for people low versus high in sense of well-being are quite large.

Unfortunately, many people do not have viable options for avoiding living stressful and unhappy lives. Many have to work, often more than one job, while taking care of children or of parents who can no longer take care of themselves (or both).

Let’s face it, many people are trapped in situations that are likely to shorten their lives, and to make them less healthy on the way to their early death. What is to be done about that?

The approach we take for people trapped by circumstances has been to find strategies that preserve well-being, no matter how harsh life may be. Aaron Antonovsky even identified attitudes that helped  people in Nazi concentration camps to survive and do well after their torment came to an end. We must learn from them and have faith that there are pathways even out of the nastiest of circumstances.

Our regular readers will understand that we prefer changing circumstances. Stay away from stress if you can.  But when that option is unavailable, fall back to neutralizing the potentially deadly consequences.

Many of our previous posts describe well-established techniques for reducing the effects of stress. For example, you can simply listen to the audio we provided in this post. Or you can use the mindfulness meditation technique we described here. These are excellent starting points, and are likely to surprise you with their power to help you through distressing situations.

Even a little time spent practicing these techniques can have dramatic effects on mental and bodily reactions to stress. All of us should have such techniques as a routine part of our lives.
