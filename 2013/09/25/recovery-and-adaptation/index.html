Recovery and Adaptation

Posted on September 25, 2013 by Jerry and Debbie Sheridan

I think it would be hard for anyone who spent even a few years probing deeply into research on stress to deny that it can really damage a person, both psychologically and physically. Reactions to stress are not just “in your head”. They are palpable.

Stress is far more important to health than most people think. And the more stress there is in a person’s life, the more likely it is that she or he will become ill.

But the load of stress people can handle without getting sick varies a lot from one person to another. What are the differences between people who are stress resistant and those who are highly vulnerable to stress? And is there some way to guide the more vulnerable people to become more stress resistant?

Once you shift to these questions, you are moving from an emphasis on stress to an emphasis on resilience. We made that shift several decades ago. Our approach has been to identify how resilient people deal with stress, and then to ask how more vulnerable people might learn to use those patterns of resilience.

It is important to realize that stress responses are adaptive. So it is not a good idea to just try to stamp out or run away from those responses.

Here is an example of the adaptiveness of the stress response. You have probably heard of the “fight or flight” response. When you have that pattern of responses, your heart pounds rapidly and the little blood vessels in the outer parts of your body constrict.

Since your blood pressure depends on the volume of blood being pumped and the amount of resistance coming from the downstream blood vessels, your blood pressure shoots up under stress. That increases the likelihood of cardiovascular diseases such as heart attack and stroke.

However, the fight or flight responses also help you if you have to fight or flee. Blood flow shifts away from your intestines to your muscles, so you are better equipped for fleeing or fighting. It also shifts away from your skin and coagulates more easily, so you will lose less blood if you are wounded.

So whether stress responses are good or bad depends on the circumstances.

Also, it may be good for you to deliberately stress yourself in order to become more resilient.

To illustrate, it is generally believed, on the basis of much research,  both by experts and by almost everybody else, that exercise is good for you. Yet exercise is stressful, and the physiological response to exercise is identical to the fight or flight response.

To be healthy, exercise has to stay within a range that allows us to recover fully and promptly.

With regular exercise we adapt to stress. We experience a “training effect” that changes our physiology in ways that enable us to exert ourselves more and more, and yet recover without harming ourselves. Basically, exercise increases our resilience.

A bonus is that, once we get that training effect from physical exercise, we can even handle psychological stress more comfortably.

Some people are just gifted with an ability to put out more work (or take more hits) than others, and still not be permanently or seriously damaged. But virtually all of us can build up resistance to stress by gradually increasing our stress level while holding back enough that we can still recover efficiently.

In the older research literature on the stress of work, it was pretty clear that people were working themselves to death. In the United States our idea of a full work-week has long been 40 hours. And at least part of the weekends are commonly time off or at least paid extra “overtime” salaries. (That appears to be changing as the rift between the well off and ordinary middle class and laboring people grows).

In many European countries, the work week is even shorter than that.  So now instead of hearing that work kills us, we often find studies concluding that retirement kills us.

It was Aaron Antonovky’s book “Health, Stress, and Coping” that helped clarify for us the importance of adaptation to and prompt recovery from stress. He spoke of “Stress resistance resources”; those resources underlie our ability to adapt to greater levels of stress. They are the primary source of resilience.

A great deal of research on resilience has centered on psychological patterns that make us more resilient. But Antonovsky saw that as only one category of factors that make us more resilient. For example, he included cultural or communal patterns that foster healthy, happy, successful, and resilient lives.

He did his research in Israel, a part of the world that included people who had been deprived of even the most basic resources that might help them adapt. These were survivors of Nazi concentration camps.  Of course they had a life before the camps, and had been able to develop inner resilience.

By tracking down the factors that differentiated people who came out of those camps and went on to do well, often exceptionally well, he concluded that some people have resources that lead them to be resilient even when those resources are taken from them.

He went on to say that researchers should turn their attention away from stress and toward resilience. He came to believe that we should be looking into what keeps us well (“Salutogenisis”) instead of what makes us ill (“Pathogenesis”).

He maintained that stress is so common that all of us are stressed enough to get sick from it.

That means we should focus on the science of resilience, which is what we have done.
